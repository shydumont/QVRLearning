Overview of Microsoft Teams

The handful of tools that do the heavy lifting

You don't need to master all of Teams. You need four things that make customer and carrier calls run smoothly. Here's each one in plain English — what it does, why it matters for booking freight, and where to dig deeper if you want to. Read it here; the links are a bonus, not a requirement.

Start here

What a Teams meeting actually is

A Teams meeting is a scheduled (or instant) video call with chat, screen sharing, and reactions built in. You can join from your desktop app, your browser, or your phone — your customer or carrier doesn't need a Teams account to join as a guest, which matters when you're meeting a small carrier or a shipper who lives in email. Audio, camera, and a shared screen are the three things that turn a phone call into a meeting people remember.

Why a QVR rep cares: Knowing a customer can join with one click and no account removes the #1 excuse for 'let's just do a phone call.'

Engagement

Chat and live reactions

The meeting chat is a side channel that runs while you talk — perfect for dropping a rate, a load number, a link, or a quick 'got it.' Live reactions (the thumbs-up, heart, applause, and raised hand) let people respond without interrupting. Use chat to make information sticky (people can scroll back to your numbers) and reactions to read the room when cameras are off.

Why a QVR rep cares: A confirmation number in chat means the customer never says 'wait, what was that load again?' A raised hand means a carrier has a question before you lose the deal to silence.

Learn more: Chat in Microsoft Teams meetings (opens in a new tab)
Show, don't tell

Sharing your screen the right way

'Share content' lets you put your screen in front of everyone — a rate sheet, a tracking map, a load board, a one-page proposal. Best practice: share a single window or file, not your whole desktop, so notifications and unrelated tabs stay private. Close anything you don't want a customer to see before you click Share. You can also hand control to someone or annotate right on screen.

Why a QVR rep cares: A customer who sees the lane on a map and the rate in writing trusts the number more than one read aloud. And sharing one clean window keeps your other customers' info confidential.

Learn more: Share content in a meeting in Teams (opens in a new tab)
Stay in control

Mic, camera, and the controls that matter

The control bar is your dashboard: mute/unmute, camera on/off, raise hand, reactions, chat, share, and leave. Mute when you're not talking so background noise (the office, the road) doesn't bleed in. Camera on for the parts that build relationship; camera off is fine when you're sharing a screen and want focus on the content. Knowing where these live means you never fumble in front of a customer.

Why a QVR rep cares: Confidence is partly muscle memory. Reps who know their controls look in command — and customers read 'in command' as 'reliable.'

Joining a Teams Meeting

Join clean, every time

Now that you've met the tools, here's how to get into a meeting without the awkward "can you hear me?" scramble. Follow these six steps and you'll show up ready — which is exactly the impression you want a customer to have before you've said a word.

Two minutes before, run the QVR pre-flight:

  • Internet connection is stable (close the VPN-heavy downloads).
  • Microphone tested — you can hear yourself in the device settings.
  • Camera framed — face centered, light in front of you, not behind.
  • Notifications off — close personal chats and other customers' files.
  • Your one screen ready to share (rate sheet, map, or proposal) is open in a single window.

    Tips for a Teams Meeting

    Now make the call work for you

    Joining is table stakes. Running a meeting that actually moves freight — that's the skill. These six habits separate a forgettable call from one that ends with "yes, send the rate con." None of them take extra time. They just take intent.

    Open with a purpose, not a "so…"

    Do this: Start with one sentence that names the goal. "Thanks for hopping on — my goal today is to get you covered on the Dallas–Atlanta lane at a rate that works."

    Reason: People relax when they know where the call is going. Vague openings make customers wonder if you're prepared.

    Share one clean screen.

    Do this: Show the rate, the map, or the one-pager — a single window, notifications off.

    Reason: Seeing the number makes it real and makes you look organized. A messy desktop does the opposite.

    Use chat for anything they'll need later.

    Do this: Drop load numbers, rates, links, and next steps into the meeting chat as you talk.

    Reason: Customers scroll back to chat. Spoken numbers evaporate; written ones close deals.

    Read the room with reactions and questions.

    Do this: Watch for raised hands and reactions; pause and ask "What's your read on that rate?"

    Reason: Silence isn't agreement. Inviting input surfaces the objection while you can still handle it.

    Mute when you're not talking.

    Do this: Stay muted unless you're speaking, especially from a busy office or the road.

    Reason: Clean audio signals professionalism. Background chaos signals "this rep is distracted."

    Close with a clear next action.

    Do this: End by naming exactly what happens next and who owns it. "I'll send the rate con in five minutes — can you sign and return it today?"

    Reason: A meeting without a next step is just a chat. The ask is what turns the conversation into a booked load.

    Camera on for the relationship moments; camera off is fine while you present so focus stays on the screen.
